Understanding Tax

⚖️ Direct Tax

  • Levied on income or wealth of a person
  • Burden cannot be shifted to another person
  • Payer and bearer are the same person
  • Example: Income-tax

🔄 Indirect Tax

  • Levied on price of goods or services
  • Burden can be passed on to another person
  • Incidence is shifted to the consumer
  • Examples: GST, Customs Duty
🏷️ Types of Taxes

Taxes are the "cost of living in a society" — levied by governments to fund common welfare expenditure.

Constitutional Basis — Article 265 & Article 246 Article 265: "No tax shall be levied or collected except by authority of law." Article 246 (read with the Seventh Schedule) splits law-making power into the Union List (Parliament's exclusive domain), State List (State Legislature's exclusive domain), and Concurrent List (both can legislate). 📘 Income-tax Act, 1961
  • Extends to the whole of India; came into force on 1st April 1962
  • Contains Sections 1–298 and Schedules I–XIV
  • A section may have sub-sections or clauses/sub-clauses
  • Clause: independent part of a section — not related to other clauses. E.g. clause (1A) of Sec 2 defines "agricultural income", clause (1B) defines "amalgamation" — each independent of the other.
  • Sub-section: related parts of a section that together complete one concept. E.g. Sec 5(1) defines scope of total income of a resident, Sec 5(2) of a non-resident — read together for the complete picture.
  • Proviso: spells out exception(s)/condition(s) to a provision — where it would not apply, or apply with modification. E.g. proviso to Secs 80GGB/80GGC disallows deduction for cash donations to political parties.
  • Explanation: gives clarification on a provision. E.g. Explanation below Sec 80GGC clarifies "political party" means one registered u/s 29A of the Representation of the People Act, 1951.

Section 3 · Section 4

Charge of Income-tax

⚡ Section 4 — The Charging Section

This is the backbone of income-tax law — the most operative provision of the Act. The tax liability of a person springs from this section. Tax is charged:

  1. At rates prescribed for the year by the Annual Finance Act or the Income-tax Act, 1961, or both
  2. On every person specified under Section 2(31)
  3. On total income earned during the Previous Year — not the Assessment Year (subject to exceptions u/ss 172, 174, 174A, 175, 176)
  4. In accordance with and subject to the various provisions contained in the Act

A "person" includes an individual, HUF, AOP, BOI, a firm, a company, etc.

💰 Total Income & Tax Payable

Income-tax is levied on an assessee's total income, computed strictly as per the provisions of the Income-tax Act, 1961. The full procedure is the 15-step computation process covered in the next topic.

⚠️
Exceptions to "Tax on PY income, charged in AY": Sections 172, 174, 174A, 175, and 176 — income of a PY may be assessed in the PY itself (covered in detail under "PY & AY Concepts").
📝 Return of Income
  • The format in which an assessee furnishes information about total income and tax payable, as notified by the CBDT
  • Particulars required: income under each head, GTI, deductions from GTI, total income, and tax payable
  • Companies and firms must mandatorily file returns before the due date
  • Other assessees must file returns subject to fulfilling certain conditions

Section 4 · Computation

15 Steps to Compute Total Income & Tax Liability

  1. 01

    Determine Residential Status

    Classify as Resident & Ordinarily Resident (ROR), Resident but Not Ordinarily Resident (RNOR), or Non-Resident (NR). Deemed Resident is always RNOR. Determines which income is included in total income — e.g. income earned & received outside India is taxable for ROR but not for NR. (Detailed in Chapter 2.)

  2. 02

    Classify Income Under Five Heads

    Salaries | Income from House Property | PGBP | Capital Gains | Income from Other Sources (residuary head). These five heads exhaust all possible types of income.

  3. 03

    Compute Income Under Each Head

    Apply charging & deeming provisions for that head. Exclude wholly-exempt incomes (e.g. agricultural income) and partially-exempt incomes (e.g. commuted pension) up to their limits. Allow head-specific deductions/allowances (e.g. municipal taxes & loan interest for let-out house property). Note: Sec 14A disallows expenses relating to exempt income, regardless of which head. Exemptions/deductions available differ by regime (115BAC default vs. optional).

  4. 04

    Apply Clubbing Provisions

    Income diverted to spouse, minor child, etc. (to reduce progressive tax burden) is clubbed back into the income of the person who diverted it. (Chapter 4.)

  5. 05

    Set-off / Carry Forward of Losses

    Intra-head set-off first (e.g. loss from one business set off against profit of another), then inter-head adjustment (with restrictions — e.g. business loss cannot be set off against salary income). Default regime: loss from house property cannot be set off against any other head. Unabsorbed losses carried forward per the Act. Brought-forward losses of one head generally cannot be set off against another head's current income. (Chapter 5.)

  6. 06

    Compute Gross Total Income (GTI)

    Aggregate net income/loss under all heads, after clubbing and set-off/carry-forward provisions.

  7. 07

    Deductions from GTI (Chapter VI-A + Sec 10AA)

    Two types: Chapter VI-A (80C, 80D, 80G, 80TTA, etc.) and Sec 10AA (SEZ units). Available only to assessees opting out of default regime. Under default regime, only select deductions allowed — 80CCD(2), 80CCH(2), 80JJAA. (Chapter 6.)

  8. 08

    Compute Total Income (TI)

    GTI − Deductions from GTI = Total Income. Round off to nearest ₹10 as per Section 288A.

  9. 09

    Apply Applicable Tax Rates

    Slab rates under 115BAC (default) or rates from the Annual Finance Act (optional regime) apply to the balance of TI. Special rates (LTCG, lottery, specified STCG, etc.) apply to specific components irrespective of regime.

  10. 10

    Surcharge / Rebate u/s 87A

    Surcharge: additional tax as % of income-tax, where TI exceeds ₹50 lakhs. Rebate u/s 87A: available to resident individuals — up to ₹12 lakh TI (default regime, max ₹60,000) or ₹5 lakh TI (optional regime, max ₹12,500).

  11. 11

    Add Health & Education Cess

    4% on (Income-tax + Surcharge), applied after the Sec 87A rebate. Leviable on all assessees — individuals, HUF, AOP/BOI, AJP, firms, local authorities, co-op societies, companies.

  12. 12

    Alternate Minimum Tax (AMT) — Sec 115JC

    Preserves the tax base against profit/investment-linked deductions claimed under the optional regime. Taxpayer can claim tax credit of excess AMT paid over regular tax in subsequent years. Not applicable to those paying tax under default regime u/s 115BAC. (Chapter 9.)

  13. 13

    Choose the Beneficial Tax Regime

    Compute TI & tax liability under both regimes (incl. AMT where applicable) and pick whichever is beneficial. Non-business assessees (Individual/HUF/AOP/BOI) can switch every year; assessees with business/professional income face restrictions on switching back.

  14. 14

    Advance Tax & TDS / TCS

    Advance tax payable in 4 instalments: 15 Jun, 15 Sep, 15 Dec, 15 Mar (based on estimated income). Presumptive-taxation assessees: single instalment by 15 Mar. TDS deducted by payer at accrual or payment (whichever prescribed) and remitted to Govt via RBI/SBI/authorised bank within statutory due dates. (Chapter 7.)

  15. 15

    Tax Payable / Refundable

    Gross tax liability − Advance Tax − TDS/TCS = Self-Assessment Tax payable, or Refund due. Round off to nearest ₹10 (Section 288B). Pay on/before the due date of return filing; refund received after filing.

Visual Flow — Computation of Total Income

  1. Determine the residential status
  2. Classify income under five heads (Salaries → House Property → PGBP → Capital Gains → Other Sources)
  3. Compute income under each head (apply charging/deeming provisions + permissible deductions/exemptions)
  4. Apply clubbing provisions
  5. Set-off / carry-forward and set-off of losses (per Act / default regime restrictions)
  6. Compute Gross Total Income (GTI)
  7. Less: Deductions from GTI
  8. = Total Income (TI)

Section 2 · Key Definitions

Important Definitions

Section 2 defines various terms used in the Act. If a term is defined in the Act, that definition governs (even if narrower/wider than common usage — e.g. "dividend", "transfer"). If not defined in the Act, refer to the General Clauses Act or dictionaries.

Section 2(7)

Assessee

A person by whom any tax or other sum is payable under the Act. Also includes: every person in respect of whom proceedings have been taken for assessment of his own income/loss/refund or of another person he's assessable for; every person deemed an assessee; every person deemed an assessee-in-default. Every assessee is a person, but not every person is an assessee.

Section 2(8)

Assessment

The procedure by which the income of an assessee is determined — by way of a normal assessment or reassessment of income previously assessed. (Detailed Assessment Procedure is dealt with at the Final level.)

Section 2(31)

Person — 7 Categories

Individual · HUF · Company · Firm · AOP · BOI · Local Authority · Artificial Juridical Person. Each is a separate unit of assessment / separate tax entity. AOP/BOI are treated as "person" whether or not formed with the object of deriving income.

Section 2(24)

Income — Inclusive Definition

Begins with "Income includes" — an inclusive, not exhaustive, definition leaving room for further inclusions. Covers profits/gains, dividends, perquisites, special allowances, capital gains, lottery winnings, PF contributions received from employees, Keyman insurance proceeds, govt subsidies/grants, and more (full list in next topic).

Section 2(9)

Assessment Year (AY)

A period of 12 months commencing 1st April every year. Income earned in the Previous Year is taxable in the immediately following Assessment Year. Always starts 1 April; always 12 months. Current AY = 2026-27.

Section 3

Previous Year (PY)

The financial year immediately preceding the AY. For a newly set-up business/profession: from date of setting-up to 31 March of that financial year. If a new source of income arises mid-year, PY starts from that date and ends 31 March. Current PY = 2025-26.

Section 2(1A)

Agricultural Income

Very wide definition — covers income of cultivators AND landholders who rent out land. May be received in cash or kind. Arises via (1) rent/revenue from land used for agriculture, (2) income from agricultural operations/processing/sale of produce, or (3) income from a farm building connected with agricultural operations. (Full detail in dedicated topic.)

Section 2(25A)

India

Includes: the territory of India per Article 1 of the Constitution; territorial waters, seabed and subsoil underlying such waters; continental shelf; exclusive economic zone; any other specified maritime zone; and the air space above India's territory and territorial waters.

🏠 HUF — In Depth
  • Members called co-parceners — related to each other and the head of family (Karta); members within four degrees including the Karta
  • A Hindu Coparcenary = persons who acquire an interest in joint family property by birth
  • Daughters have coparcenary status since 6th September 2005 — can claim partition just like a son; rights equal to a son's

Schools of Hindu Law

📍 Dayabhaga School

  • Prevalent in West Bengal & Assam
  • Nobody acquires right/share by birth while the family head is living
  • Children acquire right/share only on death of the father
  • Father and his brothers are the coparceners

📍 Mitakshara School

  • Prevalent in the rest of India
  • One acquires right to family property by birth, not by succession
  • Applies irrespective of whether elders are living
  • Every child born in the family acquires a right/share
🏢 Company — Classification

Domestic vs. Foreign Company

  • Domestic company [Sec 2(22A)]: An Indian company, or any other company that has made arrangements for declaring & paying dividends (incl. on preference shares) within India out of income taxable in India
  • Indian company [Sec 2(26)]: Formed & registered under the Companies Act (1956/2013) with its registered/principal office in India. Also includes specified corporations (e.g. Financial Corporation, State Road Transport Corporation), CBDT-declared institutions, and companies registered under laws in force in J&K, Dadra & Nagar Haveli, Daman & Diu, Pondicherry, Goa, etc. — provided office is in India
  • Foreign company [Sec 2(23A)]: A company which is not a domestic company

Widely Held vs. Closely Held [Sec 2(18)]

A company is "widely held" (public substantially interested) if it satisfies any of these:

  • Owned by Central/State Govt or RBI, or ≥40% shares held by Govt/RBI/RBI-owned corporation
  • Registered u/s 8 of Companies Act 2013 (promoting commerce, art, science, education, charity, etc.) and not distributing dividends
  • No share capital and declared by CBDT to have substantial public interest
  • Nidhi or Mutual Benefit Society declared under Sec 406, Companies Act 2013
  • ≥50% voting power held by one or more co-operative societies throughout the PY
  • Public limited company whose shares are listed on a recognised stock exchange as on the last day of the relevant PY, OR ≥50% (40% for specified industries) voting power unconditionally held throughout the PY by Govt/Statutory Corporation/widely-held company/its wholly-owned subsidiary

Closely held company = any company not meeting the above (all private limited companies fall here).

Concept of Income — Five Key Distinctions

Regular vs. Casual Receipt

Income generally means a periodic return expected to accrue regularly from a definite source. However, even certain casual receipts (not arising regularly) are treated as income — e.g. lottery winnings, crossword puzzle prizes.

Revenue vs. Capital Receipt

Income normally refers to revenue receipts; capital receipts aren't usually "income". But the Act specifically includes certain capital receipts within the definition — e.g. capital gains on sale of capital assets like land, jewellery.

Net vs. Gross Receipt

Income = net receipts, not gross — arrived at after deducting expenditure incurred to earn it (as prescribed under the Act for each head). Some businesses/professions are taxed on presumptive basis (% of gross receipts).

Due Basis vs. Receipt Basis

Taxable on due or receipt basis depending on the method of accounting (cash or mercantile) regularly employed, for PGBP & Other Sources heads. Some receipts (e.g. interest on compensation/enhanced compensation) are taxable only on receipt basis.

Application vs. Diversion of Income

Application = discharging an obligation (gratuitous/self-imposed) after income reaches the assessee — taxable in the applier's hands. Diversion (by an overriding title, before income reaches the assessee) is not taxable in the collector's hands — they merely collect on behalf of the person to whom it's payable.

Capital Receipts vs. Revenue Receipts

The Act taxes income, not capital — so distinguishing capital from revenue receipts is essential. No single test is universally decisive; each case turns on its facts. Certain capital receipts (e.g. compensation for termination/modification of services, capital gains) are specifically brought within the definition of income.

🔍 Tests to Distinguish Capital vs. Revenue
  • Fixed vs. Circulating Capital: Receipt referable to fixed capital (assets held for making profits) = capital receipt (not taxable); referable to circulating capital (turned over to yield income) = revenue receipt (taxable)
  • Capital asset vs. trading asset: Profit on sale of a capital asset → taxable as capital gains (Sec 45). Profit on sale of a trading asset (in regular course of business, or an adventure in the nature of trade) → taxable as business income (Sec 28)
  • Shares/securities sale profit: Depends on motive of acquisition — as investor/controlling-interest seeker = capital nature; as a dealer (stock-in-trade) = revenue nature; with speculative motive = revenue but dealt with separately. Note: Securities held by an FII/FPI per SEBI regulations are treated as a capital asset even if technically stock-in-trade — gain taxable as capital gains.
  • Single transaction can be "business": Even one transaction may be an adventure in the nature of trade (repetition not required) — e.g. bulk purchase + bulk sale. Whether profit is capital or revenue depends on conduct, nature/quantity of article, nature of operations, capital/revenue account, and other circumstances
  • Liquidated damages: If directly/intimately linked to procuring a capital asset (delaying the profit-making apparatus) → capital receipt
  • Compensation on termination: Agency being the only income source → capital receipt but taxable u/s 28(ii)(c). One of several agencies terminated → revenue receipt (one loss offset by other agency profits). Employment termination compensation → capital receipt but taxable as profit in lieu of salary [Sec 17(3)] or other sources [Sec 56(2)(xi)]
  • Gifts: Normally a capital receipt in the recipient's hands — but property received without/inadequate consideration is brought to tax u/s 56(2)(x) (except from relatives)
🎯
Quick Example: A banker's dealing in forex, a share broker's purchase of shares on own account — these are revenue receipts (ordinary course of business). But building/land in a share-trader's hands is a capital asset, while the same is stock-in-trade for a property dealer.

Section 2(1A) · Section 10(1)

Agricultural Income

A very wide definition — covers income of cultivators and landholders who rent out land. May be received in cash or kind. Arises in three possible ways:

① Rent or Revenue from Land

Rent/revenue derived from land situated in India and used for agricultural purposes. Three conditions must be satisfied:

  • Rent or revenue should be derived from land
  • Land must be situated in India (foreign agricultural land income is fully taxable)
  • Land must be used for agricultural purposes

Rent = amount received (money or kind) by one person from another for the right to use land — ownership not necessary; rent received by an original tenant from a sub-tenant also qualifies. Revenue is broader than rent — includes other income, e.g. fees for renewal of a land lease.

② Income from Land by Agriculture / Processing / Sale

(a) Agriculture

Not defined in the Act — broadly, cultivation involving human skill & labour on land. Involves Basic Operations + Subsequent Operations:

Basic Operations

  • Operations absolutely necessary for effectively raising produce from the land — e.g. tilling, sowing

Subsequent Operations

  • Performed after the produce sprouts (weeding, digging, etc.) — agricultural only if in conjunction with and continuation of basic operations
💡
Nursery income — Per Explanation 3 to Sec 2(1A), income from saplings/seedlings grown in a nursery is deemed agricultural income, whether or not basic operations were carried out on land.

Note: "Agriculture" cannot be extended to dairy farming, livestock breeding/rearing, butter/cheese making, or poultry farming — these have only a distant relation to land.

(b) Process to Render Produce Fit for Market

Income from a process ordinarily employed by a cultivator/rent-receiver to make produce saleable (thrashing, winnowing, cleaning, drying, crushing, etc.) is agricultural income — produce must retain its original character (unless no market exists for the raw form). If marketing process is applied to produce sellable in raw form anyway, income is partly agricultural, partly business.

(c) Sale of Agricultural Produce

Sale income by the cultivator/rent-receiver is agricultural if from land in India used for agriculture. If the produce undergoes processing beyond the "ordinary" process, the sale income is partly agricultural, partly non-agricultural. Where produce like tea, cotton, tobacco, sugarcane undergo manufacturing and the manufactured product is sold — profit consists of both agricultural income (exempt) and business income (taxable).

Rules 7, 7A, 7B & 8 of the Income-tax Rules, 1962 apportion income between agricultural and business components.

📐 Rule 7 — General Rule (Other Products, e.g. Sugar)

Where income is partly agricultural, partly business: deduct the market value of agricultural produce (raised by the assessee/received as rent-in-kind) used as raw material in the business. No further deduction for cultivation expenses.

Determining market value:

  • If sellable in raw/ordinarily-processed form: average price at which sold during the relevant PY
  • If not ordinarily sellable raw: total of — expenses of cultivation + land revenue/rent paid for that area + a reasonable profit margin as determined by the Assessing Officer

Income from Farm Building

Income from a farm building owned/occupied by the rent-receiver or cultivator, connected to land where agricultural processing occurs, is agricultural income — provided:

  • The building is on or in the immediate vicinity of the land, and
  • The occupant requires it (by reason of connection with the land) as a dwelling house or store house, and
  • EITHER the land is assessed to land revenue in India / subject to a local rate, OR (if not so assessed) the area/distance-population test below is satisfied:

Income from such farm building used for any other purpose (residential letting, business, profession) is not agricultural income.

🚫
Capital Gains on Urban Agricultural Land — NOT Agricultural Income Per Explanation 1 to Sec 2(1A), capital gains from transfer of urban agricultural land are not exempt agricultural income — taxable u/s 45. Example: A sells agricultural land in New Delhi for ₹10 lakhs, gain of ₹8 lakhs over cost — this is taxable u/s 45, not exempt u/s 10(1).

Indirect Connection with Land — Not Agricultural Income

Non-agricultural income does not become agricultural merely due to an indirect connection with land. Illustrative cases:

  • A rural society selling butter made (factory process) from members' cream — not agricultural; butter-making is a factory process separate from the farm
  • A managing agent's commission calculated on company profits (part agricultural) — not agricultural; it's remuneration under a personal-service contract
  • Surplus milk sold regularly from cows kept on agricultural land for manure/farming purposes — not agricultural if sales are a regular commercial proposition
  • Forest trees of spontaneous growth (unaided by human skill/labour) — sale income is not agricultural, even if some forestry nursing/fostering was done

  • Rent from land used for grazing cattle (for agricultural activities)
  • Income from growing bamboo

❌ Non-Agricultural Income

  • Income from breeding of livestock
  • Income from poultry farming
  • Income from fisheries
  • Income from dairy farming

Section 3 · Section 2(9)

Previous Year & Assessment Year

📅 Previous Year (PY) — Sec 3

  • The financial year immediately preceding the AY
  • Income earned in the PY is taxed in the following AY
  • New business/profession: PY = date of setting up → 31 March of that financial year
  • New source of income arising mid-year: PY = date source arises → 31 March
  • Current PY = 2025-26

📅 Assessment Year (AY) — Sec 2(9)

  • A period of 12 months commencing 1st April every year
  • Year in which income of the PY is assessed to tax
  • Always starts 1 April; always exactly 12 months
  • Current AY = 2026-27
📌 Worked Examples
  • A is running a business from 1993 onwards → for A.Y. 2026-27, the Previous Year is 1.4.2025 to 31.3.2026
  • A Chartered Accountant sets up profession on 1st July, 2025 → for A.Y. 2026-27, the Previous Year is 1.7.2025 to 31.3.2026

General Rule vs. Exceptions

📜
General Rule: Income of a Previous Year is assessed in the Assessment Year following the Previous Year. E.g. income of P.Y. 2025-26 is assessed during A.Y. 2026-27.

In a few cases — to protect the interests of revenue — this rule does not apply, and income is taxed in the same previous year it is earned:

Section 172

Shipping Business of Non-Resident

A non-resident's ship carrying passengers/livestock/mail/goods from an Indian port may leave only after tax is paid or arrangement made. 7.5% of freight paid/payable (in or outside India) is deemed income, taxed in the same year it's earned.

Section 174

Persons Leaving India

If an individual may leave India during/shortly after the current AY with no intention of returning, total income from expiry of the respective PY up to the probable departure date is chargeable in that AY. E.g. Mr. X leaves for USA on 10.6.2025 with no return intent — taxed on income earned in P.Y. 2024-25 AND for the period 1.4.2025 to 10.6.2025, before leaving.

Section 174A

AOP / BOI / AJP Formed for a Particular Event/Purpose

If such an entity is likely to be dissolved in the same year or the next, and the AO apprehends this, the AO can assess income up to the date of dissolution as income of the relevant AY.

Section 175

Persons Likely to Transfer Property to Avoid Tax

If the AO believes a person is likely to charge, sell, transfer, dispose of or part with assets to avoid paying any liability under the Act, total income from expiry of the PY to the date AO commences proceedings is chargeable in that AY.

Section 176

Discontinued Business

Where a business/profession is discontinued in any AY, income from expiry of the PY up to the date of discontinuance may, at the AO's discretion, be charged to tax in that AY.

Sections 68 – 69D

Undisclosed Sources of Income

Where an assessee cannot satisfactorily explain certain credits, investments, money, or expenditure, the Act deems these as income — taxed at a steep special rate (see "Tax Rates" topic — Sec 115BBE, 60% + surcharge + cess = effective 78%).

Section 68

Cash Credits

Any sum credited in the assessee's books for a PY, with no explanation (or an unsatisfactory one) about its nature/source, may be charged as income of that PY. Unexplained loan/borrowing: explanation is deemed unsatisfactory unless the person in whose name the credit stands also explains the nature/source AND the AO is satisfied. Unexplained share capital/premium (closely held companies): similarly deemed unsatisfactory unless the resident investor explains nature/source satisfactorily. Exception: doesn't apply to SEBI-registered Venture Capital Funds/Companies.

Section 69

Unexplained Investments

Investments made in the financial year (immediately preceding the AY) not recorded in books, with no/unsatisfactory explanation of nature and source — value taxed as deemed income of that financial year.

Section 69A

Unexplained Money, Bullion, Jewellery

Where the assessee is found to own money, bullion, jewellery or other valuable article (not recorded in books) and offers no/unsatisfactory explanation of its acquisition — deemed income of that financial year.

Section 69B

Investments Not Fully Disclosed

Where investments/bullion/jewellery are recorded, but the AO finds the actual amount spent exceeds the recorded amount, and no/unsatisfactory explanation is given — the excess is deemed income.

Section 69C

Unexplained Expenditure

Expenditure incurred with no/unsatisfactory explanation of source — treated as income of that financial year. Such deemed income cannot be claimed as a deduction under any head.

Section 69D

Amount Borrowed/Repaid on Hundi

Amount borrowed on a hundi, or repaid (otherwise than via an account-payee cheque), is deemed income of the person borrowing/repaying for that PY. If already taxed on borrowing, not taxed again on repayment. Repayment amount includes interest paid.

Worked Example — Sec 69B

If the assessee is found to own 300 gms of gold (market value ₹25,000) during F.Y. ending 31.3.2026, but has recorded spending only ₹15,000 to acquire it, and offers no satisfactory explanation —

The AO can add ₹10,000 (₹25,000 − ₹15,000) as the assessee's income for that financial year.

Marginal Relief — The Concept

🛡️ Purpose

Marginal relief ensures the increase in tax (incl. surcharge) due to a total-income increase beyond a threshold does not exceed the increase in income itself. Available wherever a surcharge threshold exists (i.e. NOT available for 115BAA/115BAB/115BAD/115BAE-opted entities, since they have no threshold).

5-Step Method (illustrated for the ₹50L–₹1cr slab, default regime)

  1. Compute income-tax on total income + surcharge@10% on that tax → (A)
  2. Compute income-tax on exactly ₹50 lakhs (no surcharge)
  3. Total income − ₹50 lakhs = excess income
  4. Add Step 2 + Step 3 → (B)
  5. Tax liability (with surcharge) = lower of (A) or (B). If A > B, Marginal Relief = A − B

The same 5-step logic applies at every threshold (₹1cr→₹2cr, ₹2cr→₹5cr, ₹5cr+ for optional regime), just substituting the relevant base amount and surcharge rates.

Section 87A

Section 2(1A) · Indirect Taxation Method

Partial Integration of Agricultural Income with Non-Agricultural Income

Agricultural income is fully exempt — but the Act has a method to tax it indirectly, by raising the rate applicable to non-agricultural income. This is called Partial Integration. Applicable to Individuals, HUF, AOPs, BOIs, and Artificial Juridical Persons only — never to companies, LLPs, firms, co-operative societies, or local authorities.

✅ Two Conditions — BOTH Must Be Satisfied
  1. Net agricultural income exceeds ₹5,000 p.a., AND
  2. Non-agricultural income exceeds the basic exemption limit applicable to that person:
    • Default regime (115BAC): ₹4,00,000 irrespective of age
    • Optional regime: ₹5,00,000 (80+ yrs) / ₹3,00,000 (60–79 yrs) / ₹2,50,000 (all others)

5-Step Computation Method

  • 01

    Tax on (Non-agricultural income + Net agricultural income)

    Add the two together and compute tax on the aggregate amount using slab rates.

  • 02

    Tax on (Net agricultural income + Basic exemption limit)

    Add net agricultural income to the assessee's basic exemption limit; compute tax on this aggregate.

  • 03

    Step 1 − Step 2 = Tax Payable

    Deduct the Step 2 tax amount from the Step 1 tax amount.

  • 04

    Add Surcharge / Deduct Rebate u/s 87A

    If applicable, increase by surcharge, then reduce by the Sec 87A rebate where eligible.

  • 05

    Add Health & Education Cess @4%

    Final step — cess on the net amount from Step 4.

    2. Classify income under 5 heads
    3. Compute income under each head (permissible deductions/exemptions — note: certain deductions allowed under normal provisions are NOT permissible under default regime)
    4. Club income of spouse, minor child, etc.
    5. Set-off / carry-forward of losses
    6. Compute Gross Total Income (GTI)
    7. Deductions from GTI — only 80CCD(2), 80CCH(2), 80JJAA allowed under default regime
    8. Compute Total Income
    9. Apply tax rates (115BAC default, or Annual Finance Act for optional; special rates 111A/112/112A/115BB/115BBJ apply either way)
    10. Add Surcharge if applicable / Deduct Rebate u/s 87A if applicable
    11. Add Health & Education Cess
    12. Compute AMT if applicable (optional regime only)
    13. Compare both regimes — choose the beneficial one
    14. Deduct Advance Tax and TDS/TCS
    15. Resultant figure = Tax Payable / Tax Refundable
    👤 Person & PY/AY
    • Person includes: Individual, HUF, AOP, BOI, Firm, Company, Local Authority, Artificial Juridical Person
    • Previous Year = FY immediately preceding the AY (FY ending 31 March in which income accrued/received). New business: from setup date to 31 March
    • Assessment Year = 12 months commencing 1st April

    5 Exceptions — PY income taxed in PY itself

    1. Shipping business of non-resident [Sec 172]
    2. Persons leaving India [Sec 174]
    3. AOP/BOI/Artificial Juridical Person formed for a particular event/purpose [Sec 174A]
    4. Persons likely to transfer property to avoid tax [Sec 175]
    5. Discontinued business [Sec 176]

    📈 Rebate & Cess — Memory Card
    • Rebate u/s 87A (Default regime): TI ≤ ₹12,00,000 → rebate up to ₹60,000. TI marginally above ₹12L → rebate = tax − (TI − ₹12L)

    🌾 Agricultural Income — Memory Card
    • Exempt under Section 10(1)
    • Aggregated with non-agricultural income to determine the rate (Partial Integration) for Individuals/HUF/AOPs/BOIs/AJP only, where:
    • — Net agricultural income > ₹5,000 p.a., and
    • — Non-agricultural income > basic exemption limit

    5-Step Computation

    1. Tax on (non-agricultural + agricultural income)
    2. Tax on (agricultural income + basic exemption limit)
    3. Tax payable = Step 1 − Step 2
    4. Add surcharge / deduct rebate u/s 87A, if applicable
    5. Add Health & Education Cess @4%

    📝 Test Your Knowledge — Questions 1–11

    1. 1Who is an "Assessee"? Explain.

      As per Sec 2(7), "assessee" means a person by whom any tax or other sum of money is payable under the Act. It also includes: (a) every person in respect of whom proceedings have been taken for assessment of his income/loss, or of another person he's assessable for, or for refund due; (b) every person deemed an assessee under any Act provision; (c) every person deemed an assessee-in-default under any Act provision.

    2. 2State any four instances where the income of the previous year is assessable in the previous year itself instead of the assessment year.

      Any four of: (i) Shipping business of non-resident [Sec 172] — 7.5% of freight deemed income, taxed same year; (ii) Persons leaving India [Sec 174] — total income up to probable departure date taxed in that AY; (iii) AOP/BOI formed for a particular event/purpose [Sec 174A] — assessed up to date of dissolution; (iv) Persons likely to transfer property to avoid tax [Sec 175]; (v) Discontinued business [Sec 176] — income up to date of discontinuance may be taxed at AO's discretion.

    3. 3Whether the income derived from saplings or seedlings grown in a nursery is taxable under the Income-tax Act, 1961? Examine.

      Yes — fully exempt, not taxable. As per Explanation 3 to Sec 2(1A), income derived from saplings or seedlings grown in a nursery is deemed agricultural income and exempt from tax, whether or not basic operations were carried out on land.

    4. 4What are the two schools of Hindu law and where are they prevalent? Explain. Also, mention the difference between the two schools of Hindu Law.

      Dayabhaga school (West Bengal & Assam): nobody acquires a right/share in property by birth while the family head is living — children acquire right only on the father's death; father and his brothers are the coparceners. Mitakshara school (rest of India): one acquires right to family property by birth, not by succession, irrespective of whether elders are living — every child born acquires a right/share immediately.

    5. 5What is the difference between an Association of Persons and Body of Individuals?

      AOP requires persons to join for a common purpose/action with the object of producing income (not merely receiving income jointly). BOI denotes persons like executors/trustees who merely receive income jointly, assessable like the beneficiaries individually (e.g. co-executors, co-trustees with indivisible title/interest). Key difference: in BOI only individuals can be members; in AOP any person (company, firm, etc.) can be a member. In AOP, members voluntarily come together with a common will/intention; in BOI, common will may or may not be present.
